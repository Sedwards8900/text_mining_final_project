Snow storm snarls Portland area commutes with slippery conditions, freezing temperatures

  • Updated: Feb. 22, 2023, 10:38 p.m.|
  • Published: Feb. 22, 2023, 2:36 p.m.
163
shares
By
  • Jayati Ramakrishnan | The Oregonian/OregonLive

A February snowstorm hit the Portland metro area midday Wednesday, causing traffic jams and delays on most major roads. Transportation officials say freezing temperatures and more snow overnight could make things even more hazardous on Thursday.

Around 8:30 p.m., northbound traffic on Interstate 5 was backed up for miles, with reports of people stuck on the Terwilliger curves for hours.

Heavy traffic backed up U.S. 26 for hours, with local news station KATU reporting a 12-car pileup at the Jefferson off-ramp.

Stuck vehicles added to the havoc as commuters tried to navigate the slick roads.

The Oregon Department of Transportation’s TripCheck website, which tracks road conditions statewide, showed around 5 p.m. that a disabled vehicle at Interstate 405 and U.S. 26 was blocking traffic, and the ramp from Interstate 84 westbound to I-5 was closed because of another disabled car and weather conditions.

The region had been under a winter weather advisory since Wednesday morning for areas above 500 feet elevation, with the Oregon Department of Transportation advising drivers to plan for slippery road conditions on morning and evening commutes.

Portland Bureau of Transportation spokesperson Dylan Rivera said motorists should pay attention to road and travel conditions before they leave the house, and have a plan for every part of the journey.

“Just because it’s raining when you start your trip doesn’t mean it’ll be raining the whole time,” he said. “You could pass through areas that are slick, where there’s snow coming down and reducing visibility.”

He encouraged drivers to slow down, give themselves extra space to brake, and give vehicles a wide berth. And, if possible, to delay trips until the weather improves.

Drivers can check conditions before they leave the house using PBOT’s interactive map, which shows snow and ice routes, the forecasts for the area and views from traffic cameras in different parts of the city. Those who travel on foot or use mobility devices can also find tips on the city’s website for how to navigate the snow.

And those who use public transit should check TriMet’s service alerts before venturing out.

The transit agency announced that it was canceling its line 18, 26, 43, 51, 63, 81 and 82 buses, which serve east Multnomah County, until road conditions improve. Portions of lines 8, 17, 20 and 72 were also closed. TriMet is chaining up all its buses, but reminded passengers that buses with chains can’t drive faster than 25 miles per hour.

The MAX Green, Orange and Yellow lines were delayed due to slow, heavy traffic, and a car had blocked MAX Blue Line tracks in east Portland, causing delays.

TriMet spokesperson Tyler Graf said riders who are trying to get to a warm place would not be turned away, regardless of whether they can pay the fare.

Vancouver’s public transit service, C-TRAN, has suspended bus line 47 for the rest of the day, and has detoured route 31. Lines 2, 6, 9, 19, 30, 32 and 92 are on snow routes.

People outside the Portland area can check road conditions using Oregon Department of Transportation’s TripCheck, which has traffic cameras and route closures for the whole state.

Some typically snowy spots, like the Santiam Pass, had reports of packed snow, and ODOT advised drivers to carry chains.

The National Weather Service has issued winter weather advisories or weather alerts for most of the state, including the Oregon Coast and central and southern Willamette Valley. The weather service predicting snow and icy road conditions that could affect commuters. Drivers should carry chains or use traction tires, and to keep emergency supplies like food, water and blankets in the car in case they get stuck.

—Jayati Ramakrishnan; jramakrishnan@oregonian.com
