Officials identify victims of southeast Missouri tornado

  • Annika Merrilees

BOLLINGER COUNTY, Mo. At least five people died early Wednesday after a tornado touched down near two southeastern Missouri towns.

The tornado ripped through Bollinger County, Missouri, outside the small towns of Glenallen and Marble Hill, about 110 miles south of St. Louis, and left a path of destruction.

Multiple injuries were reported, but the extent of deaths and property damage was not clear Wednesday afternoon. Missouri Highway Patrol troopers were assisting with search and rescue efforts, according to an agency Facebook post. An aerial post from the highway patrol showed heavy damage to homes and trees in the largely rural area.

Winds in Bollinger County reached 130 mph, according to initial estimates. Those speeds would make the tornado an EF2 classification the third-lowest on a tornado scale of EF0-5.

Eric Pendergrass, who runs a funeral home in Marble Hill, said he followed the fire trucks right after the weather calmed.

You couldnt get through the town, he said. It was complete devastation. Trees are ripped out of the ground. Houses demolished. Vehicles thrown.

Pendergrass said four of the fatalities were from one family that lived in the area of a mobile home on Route 34, a main road through the tiny town.

National Weather Service meteorologist Justin Gibbs said the tornado touched down just west of Glenallen. It was on the ground for about 10 or 15 minutes.

That is the only confirmed tornado that weve got, Gibbs said. The really significant stuff looks like its pretty well confined to Bollinger County.

Gibbs noted that tornadoes are especially dangerous when they touch down late at night or early in the morning, as this one did.

Its definitely a nightmare from a warning standpoint, Gibbs said. Its bad anytime, but its especially bad at 3:30 in the morning.

A phone weather alert awakened Bobby Masters, who said debris was slamming his Glenallen home as he took shelter in his basement with his family. He recalled hearing a roar as the tornado passed.

I had never heard a tornado before. They say it sounds like a freight train and that is exactly what it sounds like, he said. The good Lord spared us, our family and our house.

Keith Lincoln, 56, who was also awakened by a phone alert, huddled in a bathtub with his wife and 18-year-old daughter and prayed: Just save us and the house. He spent the afternoon patching his roof but was thankful his prayer was mostly answered.

Chris Green, 35, found a small, black dog dead in the debris. I cant just leave it here, he said as he and his father buried the animal.

Gov. Mike Parson said Wednesday morning he planned to join emergency personnel on the ground to assess the damage and determine what resources are needed.

The Herculaneum Fire Department announced that an Urban Search and Rescue Team assembled in Festus and planned to help in the cleanup and rescue efforts. The team is a natural disaster task force organized by the Federal Emergency Management Agency.

Wednesdays storm came as tornado warnings were issued throughout Oklahoma, Arkansas, Missouri and parts of Texas as low pressure and strong winds filed strong storms.

Just a few days earlier, severe storms and multiple tornadoes hit a wide swath of the U.S., killing at least 32 people, according to The Associated Press.

A cold front pushing east from Kansas City helped Wednesdays storm move along overnight, as St. Louis was almost 20 degrees warmer than Jefferson City just before 9:30 a.m.

Jesse Bogan of the Post-Dispatch, and Jim Salter and Scott McFetridge of The Associated Press contributed to this report.
