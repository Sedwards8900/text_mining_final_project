'Record-Breaking' Storm Hits New Hampshire, But Snow Days No Longer A Given

Photo by David Lane/Union Leader
By Josie Albertson-Grove New Hampshire Union Leader | Dec 17, 2020

Thursday’s snowstorm tapered off by 2 p.m. in most of New Hampshire, leaving most of the state covered with fluffy snow — more than three feet in some locations.

“It’s a record-breaking storm,” said meteorologist Hunter Tubbs of the National Weather Service. “It’s very unusual for us to get this much snow in one storm.”

Tubbs said Thursday morning’s cold air made for lighter, fluffier snow, which made for higher accumulations.

In some areas of the state snow fell at a rate of four inches an hour. The thick snow hindered visibility on the roads, and may have contributed to some of the 271 crashes logged by the Department of Safety between 10 p.m. Wednesday and 3:30 p.m. Thursday.

Department of Safety spokesman Paul Raymond said he did not believe any of those crashes resulted in serious injury as drivers slid off roads and got stuck in snowbanks. Raymond urged people to stay off the roads if possible, and carry an emergency kit if they are driving. More information about emergency preparedness can be found at ReadyNH.gov.

Eversource spokesman William Hinkle reported no long-lasting power outages Thursday. Hinkle said that by 4 p.m. workers had restored power to about 1,000 homes and businesses that lost power.

Some towns, including Cornish, Springfield, Andover and Sanbornton, got more than 40 inches of snow, according to the National Weather Service. Manchester recorded 14.

In any other year, 40 inches of snow would have meant a snow day — but since many schools suspended in-person learning this month and nearly all are now well-versed in remote learning, a snow day is no longer a sure thing.

Manchester schools did take a snow day Thursday, though all but a handful of students are not set to go back to in-person classes until mid-January.

Londonderry schools also were not in session — the school calendar is set up to give children adequate hours of learning even with five snow days this year, according to a message to school families.

Remote school was in session in other districts, including Nashua and School Administrative Unit 16, which covers Exeter, Newfields, East Kingston, Stratham, Brentwood and Kensington.

SAU 16 would only call a snow day in the case of a power outage, according to a message to school families.

Concord students had school remotely on Thursday, too.

“Superintendent (Kathleen) Murphy would like to assure parents that she does not intend to make every snow day a remote day,” read a note on the district’s Facebook page.

But some Concord schools were switching from hybrid to remote learning this week, so Murphy decided to keep school in session to help families settle into the new routine.

“Additionally, we are very close to winter vacation, and she would like to be conservative about the number of days we might need to make up in June — we don’t know yet how tough the winter might be.”

The weather service says no other significant storms are expected this week, but meteorologists are tracking another storm system that could arrive by Christmas. That storm, however, could be more rain than snow, Tubbs said.
