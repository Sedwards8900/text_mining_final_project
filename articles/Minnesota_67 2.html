Tracking the storm: Blizzard warning continues tonight west of the Valley; Drifting snow still expected

Check back often; our StormTRACKER team will update this article as the storm evolves.

By Tim Albertson, John Wheeler, Jesse Ritka, Lydia Blume, Dillon Vogt, and Robert Poynter
April 05, 2023 at 7:10 PM

What is this?

FARGO Wednesday, April 5, 7:10 p.m.: The Blizzard warning will remain in effect through the night over eastern North Dakota west of the Red River Valley. Wind is still blowing 25-35 with gusts to 50 mph causing heavy drifting. In the Red River Valley and across northwestern Minnesota, the Winter Weather Advisory remains in effect. Exposed locations in these areas will experience winds 15-30 with gusts to 40 mph.

Conditions are much worse in the country than within more protected cities and towns. Plows clear a highway and it drifts back in within a few minutes.

Watch the following video for snow reports and a movie of the radar display of the storm system over our area.

Wednesday, April 5, 12:15p.m.: The Blizzard Warning has been canceled for the Red River Valley, but it remains in effect for areas just to the west where it is much windier. Conditions up and down the Valley are not meeting blizzard criteria, so the National Weather Service downgraded to a Winter Weather Advisory. There still will be blowing and drifting snow in the Valley, just not as serious as originally forecast. The worst conditions will remain west of the river, where visibility is the lowest.

Even with the cancellation of the Blizzard Warning, it will remain windy through the day today. This will make it tough for road crews to clean up the mess left behind from this spring storm; especially in open areas.

ADVERTISEMENT

Where roads have been plowed, there will be improvement thanks to the strong April sunshine. Many compacted snow and ice covered roads that have been plowed will be wet and slushy this afternoon. However, if the slush is not cleared by tonight, it could cause slick spots tomorrow morning with below-freezing temperatures expected overnight.

Wednesday, April 5, 8:00a.m.: The good news about today is that the snow will slowly taper off in a southwest to northeast fashion. The bad news is that the wind is picking up. We have yet to see the strongest winds of this system. Those winds will be out of the northwest today gusting around 35 to 45 mph. A spare gust up near 50 mph out across North Dakota will be possible. This of course will cause blowing & drifting snow with reduced visibility.

The wind backs down a bit overnight tonight, but gusts near 30 mph will still be possible tomorrow morning. Still be aware of blowing snow early Thursday. The wind will slowly settle throughout Thursday and we'll see peeks of afternoon sun. Those two things will help conditions improve as the day goes on tomorrow. Mostly sunny with melting weather on Friday.

Snow reports are coming in this morning. It is still snowing in many spots, so final tallies will be a bit higher. Snow showers are still possible throughout the day today. Scroll down for more:

Wednesday, April 5, 5:00a.m.: Snow is slowly coming to an end as the wind starts to pick up. There are still a few heavier bands on radar, but it's the last of the heavy snow. The heaviest snow band developed and sat just to the north and west of Fargo.

So far for snow reports we have a report from Grand Forks of 10.0", a 9.0" inch report in Luverne, ND, and we measured 5.5" at WDAY. Snow is still lightly falling in most of these areas, so stay tuned for the storm total amounts later on.

While the snow is starting to end, the wind is just starting to pick up. Wind gusts will gust top out at 40mph which will cause blizzard conditions - especially in open country. Travel is not advised for all of eastern North Dakota.

Tuesday, April 4, 10:40 p.m.: The band of heavy snow has stalled in a line from near Jamestown and Valley City northeastward through the Grand Forks area and on into the Lake of the Woods. the dry slot has also stalled from Fargo to points east and south where scattered light snow and freezing drizzle us occurring. Some of the back edge of the snow is expected to wrap around into the southern Valley and western Minnesota overnight, but this band will be lighter than the snow that fell earlier today. Again, we have slightly adjusted the snow forecast map.

ADVERTISEMENT

Wind will slowly increase tonight and Wednesday and remain strong Wednesday night into the start of Thursday. In-town travel will be possible as streets are plowed but rural areas will likely remain impassible, extremely dangerous, or simply closed probably until some time Thursday.

Tuesday, April 4. 8:15 p.m.: Basically, all roads and highways in southeastern North Dakota are closed tonight. I-29 is closed between Grand Forks, ND, to Watertown, SD. I-94 is closed between Bismarck and Fargo, ND. Wind is slowly increasing across the Dakotas, causing poor visibility and heavy drifting along with ice accumulation on roads. Drier air has build into the storm and this will limit snow totals south and east from Fargo for the next few hours. Within this drier air, lighter snow and freezing drizzle is likely while heavy snow will continue north and west of the Fargo area. As the night progresses, some of the heavier snow is expected to wrap back around into the Fargo area for some additional accumulating snow. Once again., we've adjusted the snow forecast map slightly to account for these changes.

Tuesday, April 4, 6:00p.m: A video update of road conditions deteriorating. Numerous roads along and south of I-94 are closed (Including I-94 itself). Also, I-29 is anticipated to close at 6:30p.m. Use caution if you are traveling this evening.

Tuesday, April 4, 5:35 p.m.: As the heaviest snow continues to build northward into northeastern North Dakota and northern Minnesota, drier air is beginning to cause the snow to diminish south and east from the Fargo area. The intrusion of dry air may cause a few periods of heavier snow to develop this evening across southeastern North Dakota, but the heaviest and steadiest snow is expected from west of Fargo out to Jamestown and northeastward into the Grand Forks area.

Wind will gradually increase, with blizzard conditions developing mostly west of the Red River Valley tonight.

Tuesday, April 4, 12:30 p.m.: As expected, the snow has moved northward into Fargo and Grand Forks as of 12:30.

Due to the movement of the system it will still be awhile until areas close to the Canadian border will see snow. The original 6 p.m. time frame is still on track for areas such as Pembina, Cavalier, Langdon, and Roseau.

Note that it will start snowing in northern Minnesota before it will in northern North Dakota. Here's a peek at Futurecast at 6 p.m.

ADVERTISEMENT

While snow will just be beginning up in the far north, the snow will be at its most intense this evening for areas such as Fargo, Jamestown, Crookston, and Bemidji. The wind will have picked up considerably by then as well, causing blowing and drifting of the fresh snow.

Tuesday, April 4, 9:00 a.m.: Snow has really picked up the past few hours in the southern valley.

The heavy snow will continue to move northward through the day, reaching the Canadian border by this evening. Conditions will deteriorate quickly after the snow begins. Times are of course approximate, as this is a general outlook.

The wind will pick up quickly through the day, with gusts up to 40 mph possible in the afternoon and evening. The wind combined with the heavy snow will make for near impossible travel tonight, especially when the snow is most intense. Blizzard conditions will begin at this time.

By tomorrow morning, the most intense of the snow will be over, but it will still likely be snowing. The bigger problem becomes the intensifying wind through the day, which will gust up to 50 mph and drift and blow all of the new, fresh, snowfall. Speaking of snowfall, here is the latest snow forecast map:

Tuesday, April 4, 6:00 a.m.: Good morning! Snow is falling across South Dakota this morning and will continue its trek northward today. We'll first see the snow cross over the ND/SD state line early this morning, arrive at I-94 around mid-morning, Highway 2 around midday, and the International border by the evening commute.

The snow will meet an increasing wind today out of the northeast gusting around 30 to 40 mph. The heavy snow continues tonight, and the wind ramps up even more. By tomorrow, we'll see the snow start to wrap up *slowly* from west to east. However, the wind only gets stronger for Wednesday with gusts around 50 mph.

ADVERTISEMENT

The snow is done by Thursday morning, but we still have a decent breeze. Be aware of blowing snow on Thursday morning.

In a nutshell, conditions will deteriorate throughout the day today, worsen tomorrow, and then slowly improve throughout Thursday. We'll see sunny skies and melting on Friday.

This is a heavy, wet snow; something a lot of folks call "heart attack snow." Listen to your body when shoveling and take breaks. This kind of snow can also cause power outages. Here are some tips for power outage preparation:

Monday, April 3, 9:45 p.m.: Don't expect a blizzard the moment you wake up on Tuesday, while this storm system is moving closer tonight, most will not feel the impact until midday Tuesday.

Spots in southwestern South Dakota have already seen the snow intensify with Hot Springs, SD already at 12" of snow. By Tuesday morning the snow will be near the North Dakota border and move northward as the day progresses. Tuesday night still looks to have the most intense snow falling and the wind will be increasing as well, leading to whiteouts. Intense winds continues on Wednesday even as the snow begins to exit our area, leaving behind a large swath of 8-15".

Monday, April 3, 4:30 p.m.: Much of the region is likely to be mostly shut down for a couple of days with this approaching storm. Patches of light snow and light freezing drizzle or mist will leave a few slick spots tonight, but the main event will be developing during the day Tuesday. Snow will spread south to north through the day Tuesday, and then continue off and on through Wednesday night.

As is usually the case with these big storms, there will be varying conditions across the region and also from time to time. Snow will likely be intermittent in many areas during the storm. In the end, the heaviest snow is likely within the purple band on the snow maps with more than a foot of snow likely in some areas.

ADVERTISEMENT

Wind will approach blizzard conditions in central and eastern North Dakota, mainly west of the Valley, on Tuesday, with the heaviest snow likely coming Tuesday night. Blizzard-force wind is expected to spread across much of the region Tuesday night into Wednesday, with the worst wind impacts in flatter, more open areas of the Red River Valley and North Dakota.

Monday, April 3, 12:20 p.m.: A blizzard warning is now in effect for the western half of our region, including the cities of Grand Forks and Fargo. Winter storm warnings remain in effect for the eastern portion of our region.

Snow will begin to move into the region during the day on Tuesday and will continue through Wednesday Evening before tapering off. The snow may be heavy at times, and total snowfall accumulations of 6 to 12 inches (with locally higher amounts) will be likely across most of the region.

The blizzard warning has been issued as winds are expected to increase noticeably over the next 24 hours, with gusts up to 40 mph likely by the late afternoon Tuesday. Winds may gust even higher on Tuesday Night and Wednesday. This will lead to widespread blowing and drifting snow which will greatly impact travel.

The winds will likely begin to diminish on Thursday, but gusts over 30 mph will remain possible into early Thursday evening.

FARGO Monday, April 3, 7:00 a.m.: Early this morning, counties west of the Red River Valley were upgraded to a Blizzard Warning. This includes places like Jamestown, Edgeley, Ellendale, Ashley, Bismarck, and Dickinson. A Winter Storm Watch remains in effect for Valley City to Devils Lake and points east. This includes both Fargo and Grand Forks. We are expecting an upgrade to a warning later today.
After a beautiful Sunday, today will features a cloudy sky, a chance for a few flurries or spits of freezing drizzle, temps in the 20s and 30s, and a stiff breeze. The real action begins tomorrow.

Snow will move in from south to north tomorrow morning through midday. It will be heavy at times. We'll pair that with an increasing wind out of the NE at 20 to 30 mph, with gusts up near 40 mph. Heavy snow continues through Tuesday night and into Wednesday. Wednesday will be especially tough as the wind ramps up on the backside of this storm, gusting 50 to 55 mph. Whiteout conditions are expected. Travel will be very dangerous to impossible.

Snowfall wraps up Wednesday night into Thursday, but a breeze on Thursday will keep some blowing snow around.

ADVERTISEMENT

Through all of this, an inch or more of moisture will be possible with the heaviest snow. We will closely monitor how this plays into spring flooding.

By Friday, everything is much quieter. A mostly sunny, April sky and a temp near freezing will help with melting and improving road conditions. That's the bonus to April snowstorms, they melt much quicker than December ones.

Sunday, April 2, 9:00 p.m.: This potent April snowstorm looks on track to move into our region on Tuesday. A weak cold front swings through tonight and it will be colder, cloudy and a bit breezy on Monday but travel conditions should be fine.

The snow moves in from south to north on Tuesday so it will hit the I-94 corridor first and shouldn't reach Highway 2 and areas north until midday. Winds will be increasing during the day as well from the NE at 20-30mph, gusting to 40mph on Tuesday and that would create low visibility where it is snowing.

The wind really ramps up on Wednesday on the back side of this low pressure system as the wind shifts to being from the NW at 25-40mph with gusts topping 50mph. This, in addition to snow that will be falling will create whiteout conditions and put travel to a halt. Now is the time to consider alternate arrangements if you had plans to travel Tuesday through Thursday morning.

Sunday, April 2, 12:30 p.m.: A major late-season winter storm appears to be set on bringing severe winter weather conditions to much of the area Tuesday morning through Thursday morning. Heavy snow, possibly more than a foot in some areas, along with strong winds will likely shut down much of the region for a couple of days.
A strong low pressure system is expected to move out of Colorado early Tuesday, spreading snow, sleet, and freezing rain along and north of its path. Severe thunderstorms and tornadoes are likely to form throughout the Mississippi River Valley from Iowa to the south, on the warm side of the storm.

A precise snow total forecast is still not feasible for our region because uncertainties in the track of the low as well as the placement of sleet and freezing rain, which would likely diminish snow totals in some areas. It is also not yet known how much severe thunderstorms in the storm's warm sector will reduce energy and moisture on the storm's northwestern side.
Blizzard conditions, with heavy blowing and drifting along with extremely low visibility will likely develop Tuesday and may last into Thursday morning.

Sunday, April 2, 11:30 a.m.: A strong area of low pressure will develop in the central Plains on Monday and Monday Night. This area of low pressure will then be forced out of the central Plains by a upper level trough which will move in from the Rockies. The area of low pressure will then rapidly intensify as it moves northeastwards on Tuesday, and by Wednesday morning its center will likely be over southern Minnesota.

Snow will be likely across the Valley on Tuesday, Tuesday Night and into the day on Wednesday. The snow may be heavy at times. Mixed precipitation will also accompany the snow for the southeastern portions of the region, which will result in them seeing lesser snowfall totals. However, uncertainty remains as to how far that mixed precipitation will spread across our area, and how long it will last.
The area of low pressure will also bring windy conditions for late Tuesday, Tuesday Night and Wednesday with gusts over 40 mph looking likely for many places. This will lead to areas of blowing and drifting snow across the Red River Valley, possibly impacting travel greatly.
