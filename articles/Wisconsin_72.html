    Twin Cities heat up as western Wisconsin cleans up after storm

    By Molly Guthrey | mguthrey@pioneerpress.com | Pioneer Press
    PUBLISHED: July 25, 2023 at 3:22 p.m. | UPDATED: July 26, 2023 at 4:13 p.m.

    It’ll be hot in the Twin Cities on Wednesday and Thursday.

    Just how hot?

    “We could see the hottest days of the year so far — and quite possibly of the year,” says Brent Hewett, a meteorologist with the Twin Cities office of the National Weather Service.

    Start drinking water now to prepare for this upcoming stretch, especially if you need to be out in the elements.

    “It’ll be very uncomfortable to be outside for a long period of time,” Hewett says.

    Monday’s storms

    A quick-moving storm system blew through the Twin Cities on Monday afternoon, especially impacting Rosemount in Dakota County, White Bear Lake in Ramsey County and both the city of Hudson and the village of North Hudson in St. Croix County in Wisconsin.

    The storm lifted off part of a roof of an apartment building in North Hudson, and its winds also knocked down power lines and brought down trees in the area.

    “The Red Cross is assisting with five families and that equals nine individuals,” says Laura McGuire, regional communications manager for the American Red Cross of Wisconsin. “As I understand it, the damage was at an eight-unit apartment complex and a single-family residence home. We have been in touch with most of the individuals, and we are following up with the rest of the families today.”

    The good news is that people weathered the storm safely.

    “There were no reports of injuries, which is pretty amazing,” says Hudson Fire Chief Drew Spielman.

    People impacted by Monday’s storms and needing help can contact the Red Cross at 1-800-RED-CROSS.

    Forecast

    Nearly 400 Xcel Energy customers still were waiting for their power to be restored on Tuesday afternoon, according to Xcel’s map. Hopefully, that will happen before the next round of severe weather: The metro could see additional storm activity from 8 p.m. on Tuesday into 3 a.m. on Wednesday, according to the NWS.

    Following any storms, Wednesday and Thursday are predicted to be oppressively hot in the Twin Cities, as the record-setting “heat dome” plaguing the southern United States shifts north and east.

    Highs on Wednesday are forecast from 90 to 95 degrees, and from 95 to 100 on Thursday. The heat index could make those temps feel as hot as 100 to 105 degrees, according to current NWS projections.

    However, an air quality alert that had been scheduled through 9 p.m. on Thursday has been called off.

    “Overnight thunderstorms have helped wash out much of the leftover wildfire smoke over central and southern Minnesota. Particulate AQIs are now Green (Good) south of highway US-2,” the Minnesota Pollution Control Agency said on its site. “The atmospheric cleansing will keep ozone no worse than Yellow (moderate), and the Air Quality Alert for both today (Wednesday) and Thursday has been cancelled.”

    More rain

    After another night of storms, Xcel Energy is reporting on a power failure map that more than 16,000 of its customers are without power at about 7 a.m.

    While it’s still raining in parts of the metro early Wednesday, the heat is coming — a heat advisory goes into effect at 10 a.m. and is scheduled to last until 9 p.m. on Thursday.

    “Showers and thunderstorms will dissipate today with little chance for severe storms,” the NWS reports early Wednesday on Twitter (X). “Strong to severe storms will again be possible Thu and Fri. A Heat Advisory in effect today and tomorrow. How much rain and clouds we see will have an impact on how hot we will get.”
