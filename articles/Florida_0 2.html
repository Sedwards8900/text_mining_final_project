Weather Summary for the Next 24 Hours

Weather Summary for the Next 24 Hours:

Drier air continues to filter southward across the Panhandle and Big Bend, keeping shower and thunderstorm activity isolated along the coastline and sea breeze boundaries this afternoon (15-30% chance of rain).

 Abundant moisture from an upper-level tropical feature will push northward across the southern Peninsula and Florida Keys throughout the day allowing for scattered to widespread showers and thunderstorms through the afternoon and evening across the Peninsula (40-70% chance of rain). Additional showers and thunderstorms may develop along the sea breeze boundaries during the peak heating hours of the day. While there is no organized risk for severe weather, isolated strong to severe thunderstorms producing frequent lightning, damaging wind gusts (45-60 mph), and heavy downpours. Abnormally abundant moisture will give way to several rounds of heavy and intense downpours, allowing for instances of localized flash flooding and ponding of water at times.

Hot and humid conditions can be expected as high temperatures along East Florida will reach into the low 90s, while West Florida can expect temperatures in the middle 90s by the afternoon. Triple digit heat indices (100-108) can be expected to continue nearly statewide, despite elevated rain chances and cloud cover across Central and South Florida. A Heat Advisory continues for Miami-Dade County as heat indices upwards of 106 can be expected this afternoon. Isolated portions of South Florida and the Keys may reach heat advisory criteria during portions of the afternoon.

Showers and thunderstorms will linger through the evening hours across South Florida before mostly dissipating due to the loss of daytime heating processes (40-55% chance of rain). Scattered showers could linger through the overnight and early morning hours, especially along coastal regions due to elevated moisture levels. Low temperatures overnight will fall into the middle to upper 70s, with the Keys seeing temperatures in the low 80s.

 

Tropical Weather

Eastern Atlantic: A tropical wave is located just to the southwest of Cabo Verde Islands. Some development of this system is possible later this week and into the weekend while it moves westward to west-northwestward over the tropical Atlantic. At this time, this system poses no threat to Florida over the next 5-7 days at least.

*Formation chance through 48 hours…low…near 0%

*Formation chance through 7 days…low…30%

For the latest on the tropics, please visit the National Hurricane Center (NHC) at www.hurricanes.gov.

 

Rip Currents & Marine Hazards

Rip Currents: Elevated onshore flow will create a moderate risk for rip currents along all Florida East Coast beaches. Panhandle beaches can expect an elevated risk for rip currents to continue. All West Coast beaches remain at a low risk. A low risk can be expected elsewhere. For the latest Rip Current Outlook, visit www.weather.gov/beach.

Marine Hazards: Panhandle and West Coast beaches can expect wave heights of 1’ throughout the day. Elevated onshore flow growing throughout the day will allow wave heights of 1-3’ along Florida East Coast beaches by this evening. Isolated breaking waves of 4’ can be expected within the surf zone for Atlantic facing Key beaches. Showers and thunderstorms developing along the coastline and adjacent waters may create locally choppy surf conditions.

Red Tide has not been observed above background conditions along any Florida coastline.

Coastal Flooding: There is no risk for coastal flooding today.

 

Fire Weather

Fire Weather:Drier air continues to filter southward along the Panhandle, but relative humidity values remain well above critical thresholds. Abundant tropical moisture will surge northward across the Peninsula beginning today, allowing for scattered to numerous showers and thunderstorms. This activity may be enhanced by the sea breeze interaction this afternoon. Thunderstorms will be capable of producing frequent lightning and erratic winds. According to the Florida Forest Service, there are 6 active wildfires across the state, burning approximately 22 acres.

Drought: While several portions of the Panhandle and South Florida saw heavy rainfall across regions over the last week, the Western Peninsula saw abnormal dryness. Severe drought was reintroduced along Pinellas County through Charlotte County due to streamflows remaining low and soil moisture deficits, despite several rounds of rainfall during the month of June. With above normal temperatures and little precipitation along the western Peninsula, conditions remain dry. 30-Day rainfall deficits have reached 2-4” below normal along the western Peninsula.

The Keetch-Byram Drought Index average for Florida is 179 (+9) on a scale from 0 (very wet) to 800 (very dry). There is no Florida counties with an average KBDI over 500 (drought/increased fire danger).

 

Flooding & Hydrology

Flash Flooding: The Weather Prediction Center (WPC) is outlooking a Marginal Risk (level 1 of 4) for Flash Flooding across the southernmost Peninsula and portions of the Florida Keys. Abundant moisture surging northward from an upper-level tropical feature will give way to scattered to numerous showers and thunderstorm beginning today. Widespread rainfall across the area will be capable of producing rainfall totals of 1-3” along the southern Peninsula, south of the I-75 corridor, and through the Florida Keys. Locally higher totals upwards of 4” cannot be ruled out during multiple rounds of showers and thunderstorms producing heavy and intense downpours. Urban and low-lying/poor drainage areas cannot rule out instances of localized flash flooding and ponding of water. Any training or slow-moving showers and thunderstorms will be capable of producing multiple rounds of heavy rainfall over an area for periods of time.

Riverine Flooding: Rainfall from Sunday has kept Panhandle and North Florida rivers, creeks, and waterways below flood stage. The Fenholloway River at Foley has seen water levels rise into Action Stage (bank full) from rainfall this past weekend. The typical summertime pattern is anticipated to return through the rest of the week across the Panhandle and Big Bend, however riverine flooding is not expected. Fisheating Creek at Palmdale remains within Action Stage as water levels remains steady due to afternoon shower and thunderstorm activity over the southern Peninsula. Riverine flooding is not expected. For more details, please visit the River Forecast Center.

Lake Okeechobee’s average elevation is 15.00 feet, which is within the operational band and 1.29 feet above normal for this time of year.
