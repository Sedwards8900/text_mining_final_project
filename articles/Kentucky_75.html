Nearly 17,000 without power, reports of nonfunctioning emergency alarms following severe storm

  • Sierra Marling smarling@richmondregister.com

    Sierra Marling

    Reporter

    • Author facebook
    • Author linkedin
    • Author twitter
    • Author email
  • Jun 26, 2023
  • Jun 26, 2023

EDITOR'S NOTE: This is a developing story and will be updated as more information becomes available.

A severe storm rocked Madison County in the late hours of June 25, leaving nearly 17,000 Madison County households without electricity. The storm blew across Kentucky, causing nearly 100,000 homes across the Bluegrass to lose power. Residents reported large hail and property damage as a result of the high winds.

Richmond resident Emylee Richardson said that she and her boyfriend were sitting on the couch inside of their home when they began to hear a banging noise coming from outside.

“The sounds were pretty far apart, so we thought it was something else,” she explained. "We went to look out the windows to see what it could be and then the sounds got worse. It sounded terrible, like our whole house could collapse. There was banging and the wind sounded like a freight train. That’s when we heard glass shattering from the other room, so we immediately gathered our pets and took shelter in the bathroom.”

The Register also confirmed that the metal fencing at Eastern Kentucky University’s (EKU) Roy Kidd Stadium blew into the road on Eastern Bypass.

The broken window was caused by hail. Some outlets, like Meteorologist Chris Johnson at FOX 56, reported hail as large as baseballs and winds of up to 70 m.p.h.

Another concern among local residents is that the emergency alert radios, which were sent out by Madison County Emergency Management Agency (EMA) earlier this year, were not operating correctly and did not alert them to the severe weather.

According to Berea resident Madison Noe, she received the emergency alert for a severe thunderstorm on her phone, but the radio, which sits on her nightstand, was silent.

“I’m sure it didn’t go off because I was up there and had my daughter with me when the sirens outside went off,” she asserted, adding that her power was flickering at the time.

While her family did not suffer any storm-related damage, she said that if anyone had needed to go out for any reason, or if their phones had been dead, there could have been an issue.

“I truly wish the alarm system worked better than what it has been. It’s only went off one time & I believe that’s when they sent the test alert out on it, other than that, it’s done nothing but constantly beep even with it being plugged up & new batteries!”

Another local resident, Tracy Thompson, said that he alarm also did not consistently warn her household of storms, adding that the alarm went off after one storm was already over.

She said that she received an alert on her phone and also heard the sirens outside, but she did not hear her alarm, which sat nearby, go off.

“Seems like a waste of taxpayer money to me,” Thompson remarked.
