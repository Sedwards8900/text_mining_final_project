Looking back on some of South Dakota’s most damaging ‘winter’ storms that happened in spring

SOUTH DAKOTA – According to the National Weather Service, spring snowstorms have caused quite a bit of havoc for the state of South Dakota.

In fact, some of the state’s most dangerous storms have come in the spring.

Only in Your State lists the May 3-4, 1905 snowstorm, known as “The Great Equalizer” as its second “most horrific” winter storms in South Dakota history. According to the article, three to four feet of snow was dumped in some areas, and losses at some ranches were estimated as high as 90% of their herds.

The storm from March 29, 1981, ranks fifth on the list. That storm left more than 10 inches of snow on the ground and left power lines covered in snow and ice. On that day more than 15,000 power poles were broken as a result, and many cars were overturned on I-29.

The 2008 snowstorm on April 25 that dumped 10-15 inches of snow in East River also made it to the list in the seventh spot. According to the article, snowfall records were broken and Watertown received 19 inches in less than 24 hours. Another April storm, this time in 2013, also made the list in the ninth spot. During a severe storm from April 8-10, Deadwood received 30 inches of snow.

The National Weather Service highlighted the following winter storms that occurred after March 20 as some of the higher-ranking storms to impact the entire state.

  • March 22-24, 1937: statewide snowfall average of 8 inches
  • April 2-4, 1957: statewide snowfall average of 7.3 inches
  • March 26-28, 1975: statewide snowfall average of 9.4 inches
  • April 9-18, 1995: statewide snowfall average of 14.7 inches
  • March 29- April 1, 2009: statewide snowfall average of 8.0 inches
