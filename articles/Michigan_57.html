More than 700,000 without power as ice storm wallops Michigan

By David Eggert

February 23, 2023 06:22 AM

Utilities said Thursday it would take several days to restore electricity to more than 700,000 customers after a major winter storm coated trees and power lines with ice, particularly impacting parts of metro Detroit and Southern Michigan.

"Ice storms are pretty rare in our territory, and this one was significant," said Greg Salisbury, vice president of electric distribution engineering for Consumers Energy Co. "A half an inch on a power line makes a 20 mile per hour wind as damaging as a 60 mile per hour wind would be without the ice. So it really creates a big problem in terms of the lines as well as slowing down our crews as we navigate on icy roads and seek to stay safe where we begin restoration."

DTE Energy Co. reported that 518,877 of its customers, 24 percent, had no power as of late-afternoon following the downing of some 5,000 power lines in the storm that hit Wednesday. The Detroit-based company estimated that 95 percent of those without electricity would get it back by the end of Sunday.

Consumers, which is headquartered in Jackson, said many if not most customers would be restored on or before Sunday, but others would wait until Monday. About 202,000 continued to be affected as of late-afternoon, more than 10 percent of customers. Several thousand wires were down, the company said.

A tree branch that fell on a home in Grosse Pointe Shores during Wednesday's storm puncturing the roof into a bedroom.

"Utilities across the country fear ice storms like we fear nothing else. Ice is very damaging to infrastructure and in our case we had ice that between a quarter of an inch almost to three-quarters of an inch," said Trevor Lauer, president and COO at DTE Electric. "We've not had an ice storm in the last 50 years that has impacted our infrastructure like this ice storm."

The National Weather Service said many Detroit-area communities had not seen an ice storm with such a widespread footprint since 2003.

"On behalf of DTE and all the utilities in the state, I apologize to all the customers that are having outages and are suffering through right now," Lauer said. "We realize what it means to have a loss of power at your home and at your business. What I can assure you is that we are going to do everything we can to get the power back as quickly as we can but in a safe manner."

The hardest-hit areas with the heaviest icing and most damage include Washtenaw, Livingston and Jackson counties. Others include Kalamazoo, Calhoun, Hillsdale and Lenawee counties.

Consumers said it dealt with similar ice storms in 2019 and 2013 in its service area.

"We continue to see just a little bit of challenging weather today. As the ice melts, those limbs that would have been weighed down will pop back up and they can make contact with wires. There may be some winds as the weather changes that also creates some further damage," Salisbury said. "We know this is a hardship for our customers and families around the state. Our job is to get the lights back on safely and as quickly as possible."

At Detroit Metropolitan Airport, more than 300 flights were delayed or canceled Thursday, according to FlightAware data. Hundreds of flights were affected Wednesday. The Legislature canceled session for the second straight day. Many schools were closed again.

Asked if lines should be buried underground to prevent outages, both DTE and Consumers said it is very expensive. DTE has put new infrastructure underground since the 1970s.

Lauer said it is appropriate to place some existing lines underground at some point but not in a large-scale manner

"Imagine tearing up every street and corridor. It's not like repaving a road. So first, just from an environmental aspect, I'm not sure that anybody would want us to do it if you could do it," Lauer said. "The next thing is cost. It's three times more expensive to put infrastructure underground that it is overhead. And I recognize that underground doesn't have the number of outages that overhead has. But when you have an underground outage, it tends to last a lost longer than overhead outages happen. ... If you trim trees and if you take care of the infrastructure, you get largely the same reliability from overhead or underground. This ice storm is different. Ice storms don't hit like this. This is not a common occurrence. So to spend the billions and billions of dollars, I'd have a hard time saying that the customer should invest that at this point."
