Oklahoma tornado kills mother and baby as region suffers yet another devastating set of storms

By Ginger Adams Otis
NEW YORK DAILY NEWS
•
Jun 01, 2013 at 8:38 am

A powerful brew of tornadoes and flash floods ripped across the prairies Friday unleashing pelting hail before slamming into Oklahoma City, killing a mother and baby while crushing cars and trucks along two major interstates.

Less than two weeks after a megatwister devastated nearby Moore, Friday's violent storm was one of at least three tornadoes that tore across Oklahoma City and its suburbs, trapping commuters on windswept highways amid flying debris.

The unidentified mother and her infant were killed when their car rolled over, according to local station KFOR-TV.

At least one other person was confirmed dead and the Oklahoma Highway Patrol reported multiple motorists injured, with others missing as power went out and the area was plunged into chaotic darkness.

Advertisement

One of the twisters touched down near Moore, which was decimated May 20 by a mammoth funnel storm that killed 24 and reduced the town to matchsticks.

Hurricane-force winds raged between 80 to 100 mph and motorists were urged to get off the roads as the storm bore down on neighboring Missouri.

Will Rogers Airport in Oklahoma City was shut down, forcing stranded travelers to seek shelter in its basement. The airport was among 50,000 customers to lose power.

Multiple funnel clouds were spotted at ground level as the system blew east.

It hit during the evening rush hour around 7 p.m., causing havoc on interstates 40, a major artery of the city, and 35.

Floodwaters up to 4 feet deep hampered rescue attempts and frequent lightning roiled the skies well after the main threat had moved on.

Oklahoma Highway Patrol Trooper Betsy Randolph said officers found the bodies of a woman and her baby near their vehicle. Randolph said it's not known if the woman was driving into the storm.

Hail and heavy rain pelted the metro area to the point that emergency workers had trouble responding to "widespread" reports of injuries.

"We're scrambling around," said Lara O'Leary, a spokeswoman for the local ambulance agency. "There is very low visibility with the heavy rain ... so we're having trouble getting around. "The damage is very, very widespread."

Standing water was several feet deep, and downtown Oklahoma City looked more like a hurricane had gone through than a tornado. Tornado warnings were also posted Friday night near Tulsa and St. Louis.
