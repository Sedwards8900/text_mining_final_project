Terrible tempest sweeps through Keokuk

  • Erika Barrett and Joe Benedict dgcnews@dailygate.com and editordgc@dailygate.com

    dgcnews

A severe storm lashed Keokuk Thursday morning, leaving devastation in its wake with tipped semis, blown-over power poles, and scores of fallen trees. With power outages throughout South Lee County and numerous roads blocked, the local community is rallying to clear debris and manage the aftermath.

The tempest hit at 10:20 a.m., causing several semis to tip over on 218/27 in Lee County and closing Connable Road in Hamilton. The extent of the damage prompted LeeComm to request additional assistance from state troopers. Hancock County deployed extra deputies.

Due to the storm, with winds that have been estimated to have possibly reached 120 mph, the Lee County Board of Supervisors on Friday declared a state of emergency, authorized under Iowa state statute and will execute the expenditure of emergency funds from all available sources, the invoking of mutual aid agreements and the applying of the State of Iowa for assistance.

The National Weather Service is calling the storm a derecho. The service did, however, confirm an F1 tornado touched down west of Kahoka, Missouri, but did not confirm a tornado in Keokuk. That being said, the service estimates 80-120 mph winds throughout the storm.

I guess no tacos tonight, huh? One woman was overheard joking with a friend in a Keokuk front yard strewn with debris. Amid the broken trees and wreckage, a sense of camaraderie and resilience was evident in the affected community.

The City of Keokuk reached out to citizens via its Facebook page, requesting their help to clear debris from roadways and alleys. Alliant Energy estimated power restoration by 10 p.m. Friday, urging locals to stay safe and hopeful despite the widespread damage.

Many homes were damaged as a result of fallen trees, including that of Ronnie Braggs, whose property was hit by a large tree as he arrived home with his children. There was a huge shake, Braggs said. I got everybody, and we went in the basement. The extent of the damage to their home remains undetermined.

Braggs isnt just concerned about his home. Hes worried about how his decision to stay home and focus on his home and family will impact his job.

My bosses are going to try to give me a point at my job because Im not going in today, he said. I cant be expected to go on in a day like today.

Todd Miller, 63, saw one of the worst storms in his lifetime. My buddy and I saw it was going to rain today, so we slipped out to the golf course about quarter after seven this morning we wanted to get in 18 holes before noon, Todd Miller said.

We got nine in and decided to go home. Its a good thing we came home.

After managing to get home before the storm hit, Miller described the sight of cable lines lying in his pool, downed telephone poles, and shattered windshields from fallen trees.

Melissa Derr knew the storm was approaching and decided to move her car from the street to the driveway. The storm came up so fast, Derr said. I got it parked, and as soon as I hit my kitchen door, (a tree) fell.

The tree she referred to was a massive oak in the front of her yard that splintered at the trunk and crashed into the Derrs home, damaging the roof and crushing her car.

Meanwhile, the situation at the local HyVee supermarket highlighted the communitys resourcefulness and compassion. Customers and employees alike sought shelter in the coolers when the storm hit. Shoppers like Lindsy Swindler expressed gratitude to the store for providing blankets and safety amid the tempest.

The aftermath presented a sobering sight for many, including John Davis, who returned home from HyVee to find a giant tree sprawled atop his damaged home. His wife Helen had been in the basement at the time, only realizing the extent of the destruction when she emerged.

Helen found a confetti of broken light fixtures and insulation with water spilling all over the floor.

I think the back of our addition and the garage is ruined, she said.

Other reports include trees completely blocking routes, including N. 13th, Hilton Rd, 16th and Carroll, and others. The roof of Becks has blown off.

Powerlines were snagged by trees on N. Third Street and Kilbourne and McKinley.

Uprooted, fractured, or damaged trees throughout town. You look at Rand Park, and probably 30% of the trees are roll twisted off, Keokuk resident Mike Merrick said.

The City of Keokuk has announced that due to extensive damage, Rand Park has been closed until further notice.

Broken trees have landed on cars, shattering windshields and disabling them.

Lights from baseball fields have fallen and shattered.

Metal and plastic Sheds have tumbled and landed on neighboring properties and buildings.

Nearly half of the sound wall bordering the Indian Hill neighborhood has collapsed.

Keokuk Fire Department, while admitting the winds were tornadic, insisted the storm was not an official tornado. Later reports confirmed the storm was a derecho.

KFD encouraged residents to report any downed power lines and reassured the community they were ready to respond to any emergencies, especially those with medical needs that require electricity.

Lee County Sheriff Office has made a statement encouraging folks to avoid any unnecessary travel while crews work to clean up debris and power lines.

While clean-up efforts are in full swing and power is gradually restored, the storms impact on Keokuk continues to unfold. Despite the daunting circumstances, the sense of community and spirit of perseverance is evident, embodied in Melissa Derrs words, Were going to have a little bit of cleanup, but were good. Were safe. All these things can be replaced.
