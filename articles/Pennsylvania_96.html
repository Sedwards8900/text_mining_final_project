Pennsylvania must do better to address storm water flooding | Opinion

  • Published: Mar. 31, 2022, 8:19 a.m.
By
  • Guest Editorial

By State Rep. Joe Webster

As dawn broke on Sept. 2, 2021, it was evident that the damage from Hurricane Ida would remain long after the storm clouds moved on. The remnants of a Category 4 hurricane that made landfall in Louisiana earlier that week had finally reached our area the previous night, spitting out tornadoes and flooding every inch of land in its path.

Rain swelled the creeks and rivers, and with nowhere to go the waters flooded our streets and our homes. A truly historic storm in its own right, Hurricane Ida, the 9th hurricane out of 21 for the 2021 season, resulted in the deaths of 115 persons and continues to cost the United States billions of dollars in damages.

We are now less than 100 days away from the 2022 hurricane season and Pennsylvania is no more prepared for the expected onslaught of storms and flooding than we were before Ida. Flooding and storm water remains one of the biggest weather-related threats Pennsylvania faces in the upcoming decade due to climate change.

The commonwealth is home to more the 80,000 miles of waterways, second in the nation only to Alaska, and coupled with our centuries-old cities and aging infrastructure, a surge in water can be devastating.

I have already introduced House Resolution 170 to the legislature that would order a statewide study of the economic costs of flooding on residential homes and businesses, as we currently have no way to track such information. This data - recording the areas that consistently see flooding and what storm water projects we are investing in or consistently repairing - would hold Pennsylvania accountable and inform future community development projects.

We know Pennsylvania and the federal government have doled out hundreds of millions of dollars in the past decade in our state alone for recovery efforts after weather disasters, but we lack the ability to track these funds and establish preventative measures.

To further empower Pennsylvania to combat flood waters, I will soon introduce a bill that would require counties in the state to prepare and implement a comprehensive storm water management plan based on watershed boundaries. Such plans would include proposed solutions to existing storm water runoff issues and assessments of storm water management facilities. Communities and local officials have the greatest understanding of where flooding occurs within their townships or boroughs and are best qualified to assess their needs and find solutions.

The chronic threat of flooding our state faces will only worsen in the coming decades as the planet continues to heat up. It is estimated that by mid-century the commonwealth will see an increase of precipitation of 8% while the strength of extreme storms and hurricanes remain high, resulting in devastating flooding for this region. We have the responsibility to prepare and protect Pennsylvanians to the best of our ability from these serious and looming threats.

State Rep. Joe Webster represents the 150th Legislative District in Montgomery County, Pennsylvania.
