Vt. officials say dangerous storms will complicate flood recovery

By Calvin Cutler
Published: Jul. 13, 2023 at 9:55 AM EDT

BERLIN, Vt. (WCAX) - Vermont officials Thursday urged extreme caution as another round of dangerous weather arrives Thursday afternoon, bringing more rain and high winds to already flood-ravaged areas of the state.

Thursday, Friday, and Sunday are expected to bring additional threats of flash flooding to areas where rivers are high and the ground is already saturated. The governor at a press briefing in Berlin urged Vermonters to remain vigilant and not become complacent.

Vermont Public Safety Commissioner Jennifer Morrison says the new storms on the way will likely hit Thursday afternoon between 4-8 p,m. and could rain, hail, high winds, or even tornadoes. She says the state is pre-positioning swift water rescue teams ahead of the dangerous weather although they are not likely to see the kind of flooding as earlier in the week.

Officials say one Vermonter has now died from the severe storms and flooding. The Department of Health says 63-year-old Stephen Davoll died Wednesday in his Barre home. They say it was a drowning accident.

Governor Scott and Commissioner Morrison urged people -- especially children -- to stay out of the floodwaters, which contain petroleum products, sewage, and other dangers.

Governor Phil Scott said he will submit a request Thursday to President Biden for a major disaster declaration. FEMA officials toured the state Wednesday as part of the preliminary damage assessment triggered by the president’s initial approval earlier this week.

VERMONT OFFICIALS CLOSELY MONITORING DAMS

The Vermont Agency of Natural Resources says they are closely watching the Waterbury, Wrightsville, and East Barre dams -- all of which feed into the Winooski River. Rainfall and water level data are being collected at each location for emergency planning purposes.

Earlier this week there were concerns water might reach the Wrightsville dam spillway, an unprecedented situation since the dam was constructed following the 1927 flood. But officials say they’re confident the dam can handle the expected two inches of rain coming. However, they say three inches of rain would result in a spillover of about a foot of water, which would cause minor flooding downstream.

TRANSPORTATION UPDATE

VTrans officials say 24 state roads continue to be closed out of the 81 that were initially damaged. Eight roads are partially open. Updates are provided at 511.

Officials say 49 bridges have been inspected and several of them need minor repairs. The Route 113 bridge in Vershire is closed because of damage but they hope to have it back open by July 24. The Floating Bridge in Brookfield is intact. VTrans Wednesday said the bridge was gone based on an initial report but they now say a portion of it is “inverted.” A man who lives near it says instead of having to drive down to the bridge, the water rose high enough that now you need to drive up to it. VTrans is working to determine whether that bridge can be saved.

Rail officials continue to inspect tracks in the state but Amtrak service for now remains closed for both the Vermonter and Ethan Allen Express. Amtrak wants a train that is stuck in Burlington back to its hub.

VERMONT NATIONAL GUARD LENDS A HAND

The Vermont National Guard says nearly 75 soldiers and airmen are on the ground in support of the state’s flooding response. Officials say they rescued 27 people and two dogs. The guard says they’re delivering water to Woodstock, Ludlow, Marshfield, and Morrisville among other assignments.
