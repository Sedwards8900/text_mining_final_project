PRECAUTIONARY/PREPAREDNESS ACTIONS...

Drink plenty of fluids, stay in an air-conditioned room, stay out
of the sun, and check up on relatives and neighbors. Young
children and pets should never be left unattended in vehicles
under any circumstances.

possible. To reduce risk during outdoor work, the Occupational

Strong tornado tears through Nashville, Central Tennessee early Tuesday

  • By Justin Ballard
  • Mar 3, 2020

ROCKFORD (WREX) Middle Tennessee, including the Nashville area, bore the brunt of a strong and nearly direct hit by a tornado early Tuesday morning.

As of Tuesday afternoon, 22 people were confirmed dead.

The 24th largest city in the United States, the tornado touched down just west of Davidson County, home to Nashville. By 12:45 a.m. Tuesday, the tornado had reached the northern reaches of Nashville, missing the Central Business District by just a handful of miles.

A survey team from the National Weather Service in Nashville is going to be out and about over the next couple of days to assess the damage. From the pictures and video that have trickled into the newsroom, the damage in middle Tennessee is considerable. Video from early Tuesday show numerous flashes of light as transformers blew across the city's power grid.

This tornado disproves the all too common notion that damaging tornadoes don't impact large cities. In fact, 2019 saw an astounding number of large cities impacted by strong tornadoes. Cities like Dallas, Texas, and Dayton, Ohio, were heavily damaged and millions of people were impacted.

Nashville is no stranger to tornado visits, but this particular event was an extremely dangerous one because it came in during the overnight hours. On April 16th, 1998, a F-3 tornado ripped through Downtown Nashville, causing millions of dollars worth of damage. That tornado ripped through town a little after 3 p.m. local time. Last night's tornado came through between 12:30 and 1 a.m. local time, while most people were asleep and not able to receive proper notification.

Tuesday morning's tornado took an eerily similar path to the tornado from over 20 years ago. Both heavily impacted the city's north side, but Tuesday morning's storm also brought extensive damage to areas of East Nashville. The tornado continued past Nashville, leading to a nearly 35-mile path of destruction into Wilson County.
