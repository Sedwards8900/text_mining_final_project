BREAKING NEWS (updated with photos): Storm prompts state of emergency; motorists stranded, thousands without power

  • Dec 19, 2009
  • Dec 19, 2009 Updated Jul 29, 2014

The worst winter storm to hit southern West Virginia in nearly 12 years paralyzed much of the region Friday night and early Saturday, closing major highways and leaving motorists stranded in their vehicles for hours.

Southbound traffic on the West Virginia Turnpike from Charleston and Beckley came to a standstill because of a number of accidents involving tractor-trailers.

A woman from South Carolina called The Register-Herald on Saturday morning and said her parents, trying to return home from Michigan, had been stranded since 6:30 p.m. Friday about 50 miles north of Beckley. She said her father is diabetic and that her parents were down to two oranges and a PowerBar.

She said they were also unable to turn around and go back to Charleston because of a concrete barrier between the northbound and southbound lanes.

We cant get any information (from authorities), she said.

Another woman said her son, a student at Xavier University in Cincinnati, was driving home to Roanoke, Va., when he got stranded about 11 miles north of Beckley.

Hes been sitting there 13 hours and hasnt moved at all, she said. He has no food or water, and hes cold.

Another caller said his two sisters had been stranded all night on Interstate 64.

Theyve been unable to reach anybody to tell them anything, he said.

Coal miner John Crump, who works near Mossy and lives near Coal City, was stuck in turnpike traffic beginning at about 6 p.m. Friday. He finally made it to his home around 6 a.m. today. He said traffic was "at a standstill" for nearly 10 hours on the turnpike. "It's a mess," he said from his four-wheel-drive truck while waiting. "I haven't seen anybody from the turnpike," he said about 6 hours into the ordeal.

Gov. Joe Manchin declared a state of emergency, and National Guard personnel and heavy equipment were dispatched to the turnpike just north of Beckley to move disabled vehicles.

But state Homeland Security and Emergency Management Director Jimmy Gianato said the National Guard vehicles, traveling from Charleston, were unable to get around the backed-up traffic. He said they were diverted to U.S. 19 to help with problems in the Glen Jean and Oak Hill areas, then would attack the turnpike situation from Beckley, moving north.

The roads are impassable, he said. Its a slow process.

He was hopeful that National Guard personnel would reach the stranded turnpike motorists very shortly to provide them with food, water and fuel.

Interstate 64 in Greenbrier County was said to be impassable, and emergency officials were urging the public to stay off roads throughout the region to give crews the opportunity to plow and treat them.

Snowfall amounts included 24 inches in Oak Hill and 20 in Beckley. Marlinton in Pocahontas County had 26 inches and an Alderson resident said his community had every bit of 2 feet of snow.

Emergency shelters were set up throughout the region, including the Beckley Dream Center on Pinewood Drive; the Lewis Community Center, Oak Hill High School and the Pax Volunteer Fire Department in Fayette County; the Sandstone and Green Sulphur Springs volunteer fire departments and Elton Church and Lick Creek Baptist Church in Summers County; and Rhema Christian Church in Greenbrier County.

Downed trees and power lines were also reported.

Appalachian Power reported that 4,500 customers in the Beckley area were without electricity Saturday morning, 4,700 in the Pineville area and 1,480 in the Rainelle area.

As of noon Saturday, 3,890 Allegheny Energy customers in Nicholas County were without power, 1,580 in Webster County, 232 in Summers County, 126 in Greenbrier County and 101 in Pocahontas County.

This storm is the worst snow event to hit southern West Virginia since January 1998 when heavy snow caused 90 roof collapses in the Beckley area.
