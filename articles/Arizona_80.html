Correction to a Raiden Storm quote about the Arizona monsoon season forecast

Arizona Weatherman2023-05-31

Journalistic integrity is essential to the author, and accurate information in an article is paramount. Sometimes, unintentional mistakes occur when gathering facts and quotes for a given article. A good journalist will correct the story to make it accurate for the public. The Arizona Weatherman takes this commitment very seriously, and when he makes a mistake, he will own up to it and correct it.

Arizona Weatherman, in a recent article, Updated Arizona monsoon forecast after discussion with Raiden Storm unintentionally misquoted Raiden Storm. The incorrect quote from the article was, “The monsoon will more likely kick off in late June and have fewer overall thunderstorms but have more dynamic severe weather associated with the upcoming monsoon.” Additionally, as stated in the article, Raiden Storm believed there would be average to below-average rainfall for the upcoming Arizona monsoon 2023 season.

According to a more recent Raiden Storm article, it contradicted the above quote. The article, 2023 Arizona Monsoon Forecast Released; Strong Hurricane El Nino Influenced Season Expected, “Based on the warm waters now, analog seasons for this year’s monsoon will be 1972, 1982, and 1997, all of which had an above average monsoon.” He is instead forecasting above-average rainfall for Arizona this upcoming monsoon season.

The Arizona Weatherman’s monsoon forecast is average to below average based on the statistical likelihood from past seasons with El Nino events over Arizona that 73% of El Nino monsoons tend to be average or below. Additionally, three above-average rainfall rate monsoons in Arizona are also rare. However, the Arizona Weatherman is not a climatologist but a meteorologist, with the most experience forecasting weather within the week.

Could Arizona get a few hurricanes and tropical storms recurving and moving up the Baja into Arizona and increasing the overall rainfall? Sure, it could happen, and Raiden Storm believes this is the case according to his forecast. Additionally, NOAA is forecasting an above-average tropical season for the Eastern Pacific as seen in the above picture. This could set the stage for tropical moisture impacting Arizona.

As he pointed out in that above article, “NOAA is going dry for the monsoon yet again as per their latest summer forecast. Hot and Dry yet again. But remember, they did this the last couple seasons as well and what happened? Opposite happened is what, and that will happen yet again.” Furthermore, Raiden Storm’s forecast was correct for the last two monsoon seasons. Personally, the author is hoping he is right yet again. Arizona could use three more above-average seasons to start to overcome the 20-year drought conditions for the state.

The bottom line, no matter if the 2023 Arizona monsoon season is above or below average rainfall for the state, both Raiden Storm and Arizona Weatherman will be watching and tracking the tropical potential and forecasting when and if these events impact the state. Arizona Weatherman wants to ensure that accurate information is getting out to the public and when a mistake occurs to correct it and do better in the future.

Arizona's deadly dust storms: Haboobs prove treacherous for motorists

What is a haboob? The term is from an Arabic word in origin, meaning to blow. According to the American Meteorological Society Dictionary, it is “An intense sandstorm or duststorm caused by strong winds, with sand and/or dust often lofted to heights as high as 1500 m (~5000 ft), resulting in a “wall of dust” along the leading edge of the haboob that can be visually stunning.” The definition also informs that this storm results from a thunderstorm with an outflow of strong winds that pick up dust and sand and carry it some distance. It can be associated with a cold front, dry line, or another synoptic feature that initiates thunderstorm activity. It also rapidly reduces visibility to near zero for a period of minutes to hours.
