As hurricane season starts, a look back at 10 recent Louisiana storms

  • Slade Rand, NOLA.com | The Times-Picayune
  • Jun 1, 2017
  • Jun 1, 2017 Updated Jul 22, 2019
  • Facebook
  • Twitter
  • WhatsApp
  • SMS
  • Email
  • Facebook

By Slade Rand, NOLA.com | The Times-Picayune

Hurricane season starts Thursday (June 1), and as always, government and weather officials are reminding residents that it just takes one storm to disrupt lives and livelihoods. Here's a look back at recent storms that have hit New Orleans and their impacts.

Hurricane Andrew - August 1992

Andrew became the first named Tropical Storm of the 1992 season on Aug. 17, 1992 and ultimately strengthened into a Category 5 hurricane. The storm first crossed Florida near its southern tip and then re-entered the Gulf, growing stronger until it touched down near Morgan City.

The storm reached its highest wind gusts in St. Mary Parish and gale force winds battered the state for 12 hours. Eight Louisianians died. About 182 million fish died in the Atchafalaya River Basin due to the storm and the state’s sugar cane crop took $200 million worth of damage.

Morgan City sustained heavy wind damage during Hurricane Andrew. In Florida's Miami-Dade County, more than 25,000 houses were destroyed and nearly 100,000 homes were significantly damaged. Andrew was the most expensive natural disaster in the country’s history at the time, causing an estimated $26.5 billion in damage.

Hurricane Isidore - September 2002

Hurricane Isidore formed September 18, 2002 as the second named hurricane of the 2002 season. As Isidore picked up strength and speed, Caribbean and coastal cities prepared for significant damage.

The storm moved slowly across Cuba and grew to a Category 3 hurricane as it approached the Yucatan Peninsula. Isidore’s peak wind speed reached 120 mph before landfall on the Yucatan Peninsula. Instead of rushing towards the U.S. coast, the hurricane subsided to a strong tropical storm on its path towards Louisiana.

The storm approached Louisiana with wind speeds of 65 mph and made landfall west of Grand Isle as a tropical storm. Most of the estimated $330 million in damage occurred in Louisiana crop fields, where the storm brought torrential rainfall on its northern path.

The hurricane was responsible for four drowning deaths in the U.S. and one indirect death in Mississippi. The name Isidore was retired for use as an Atlantic Hurricane, and was replaced by Ike in 2008 when another hurricane headed towards Louisiana.

Hurricane Lili - October 2002

Hurricane Lili was the first hurricane-force storm to hit the U.S. since Hurricane Irene made landfall in Florida in 1999. It reached its peak intensity over the Gulf of Mexico, between Cuba and the U.S. coast. Lili caused an estimated $860 million in damage across the country.

Lili originated as a tropical depression off the west coast of Africa on Sept. 16, 2002. After passing over Cuba, Lili grew to a Category 4 storm in the Gulf of Mexico and caused a shutdown of the majority of the 4,000 oil and gas facilities in federal waters. Eight hundred facilities were in the storm’s path but only six took substantial damage; two were fully submerged.

On Oct. 3, Lili made landfall on the Louisiana coast with recorded 90 mph winds. Despite its weakened state compared to its time over the Gulf, a storm surge and heavy rain breached levees in Montegut and Franklin and flattened sugar cane fields across the state. The American Insurance Services Group reported $415 million in damage to U.S. insured property in Louisiana. The hurricane was indirectly responsible for two deaths in Louisiana, and directly responsible for 14 along its path.

Tropical Storm Bill - June 2003

Tropical Storm Bill formed south of Louisiana in the Gulf of Mexico. Bill’s maximum sustained winds were around 60 mph before its landfall, as it did not have much space to intensify. The storm was branded by flooding, tornadoes and widespread rainfall for nearly a week across the state.

Bill produced five recorded tornadoes in its wake, one of which struck Reserve and heavily damaged 20 mobile homes injuring four people. Bill’s path through central Alabama saw it weaken to a tropical depression across the eastern U.S.

Louisiana forecasters canceled the hurricane watch in place as Bill approached landfall, but the National Weather Center warned of a “dangerous flood threat from rainfall and storm surge.” Bill flooded roads in Grand Isle, almost 100 miles east of its landfall. Levees along the Grand Isle coast took damage as well. Bill caused an estimated $30 million in damages.

Hurricane Cindy - July 2005

Cindy formed as a tropical cyclone on July 3, 2005 below the Yucatan Peninsula and headed Northwest towards the Louisiana coast. Once over the Gulf of Mexico, the warm waters helped elevate Cindy to a tropical storm. This made Cindy the third named storm of the season, and it eventually became the season’s first hurricane.

Cindy grew to near-hurricane strength as the storm made landfall southwest of Grand Isle, eroding the coast there.

Cindy made landfall in Louisiana on July 6 and passed over New Orleans. It subsided greatly over the city and made its way to Georgia as a frontal wave. Most of the storm's damage came from tidal flooding along the coast and inland flash flooding. Cindy caused a widespread blackout in New Orleans and knocked down trees in City Park.

The fading storm moved northeast on its last legs, flooding streets in New Orleans and spinning towards Alabama with a few tornadoes in tow.

Hurricane Katrina - August 2005

Katrina formed as a tropical depression in the Bahamas on Aug. 23, 2005 and grew to a tropical storm within 24 hours. The storm became a Category 1 hurricane before moving from the Bahamas to its initial U.S. landfall on the Florida coast. Katrina crossed past Florida and into the Gulf of Mexico where it quickly grew to a Category 5 hurricane sustaining 175 mph winds. The devastating storm approached southeast Louisiana as it transitioned to a Category 3 hurricane.

On Monday, Aug. 29, Hurricane Katrina made landfall again near the Louisiana-Mississippi border as a Category 3 hurricane. Though the storm had weakened, New Orleans was already beginning to flood in the early morning hours as sections of the Industrial Canal's walls fell and storm surge overwhelmed the Intracoastal Waterway. From there, the water kept coming: For a complete timeline of events click here. As protection systems failed, about 80 percent of the city was ultimately flooded.

Hurricane Katrina is the costliest hurricane to ever hit the United States, responsible for an estimated $108 billion in damage and nearly 1,800 fatalities. During the storm’s aftermath, FEMA director Michael Brown resigned under national criticism of his crisis management.

Hurricane Rita - August 2005

Hurricane Rita was the third Category 5 storm of the 2005 season. It formed as a tropical depression on Sept. 17, moving over the Bahamas and through the Florida Strait. By the time it reached the Gulf of Mexico, it had spun into a Category 2 hurricane.

In the Gulf it quickly intensified, becoming a Category 5 hurricane within about 24 hours. It reached peak intensity on Sept. 22 with winds of 180 mph. Rita made landfall just east of the Texas and Louisiana border on the morning of Sept. 24 as a Category 3 storm, with maximum sustained winds of 115 mph. It continued traveling north, remaining a tropical storm until it reached northwest Louisiana.

Rita's damage was extensive, including to areas that had been spared by Hurricane Katrina just a few weeks earlier. Storm surge flooding of 10 to 15 feet above normal tide levels was reported along Louisiana's southwestern coast. The storm also spawned an estimated 90 tornadoes over the southern United States, according to NOAA.

In all, NOAA puts the death toll from Rita at seven people in the U.S. The storm caused $10 billion in damage.

Hurricane Gustav - September 2008

Under the shadow of Hurricane Katrina’s three-year anniversary, coastal cities were on high alert as a tropical depression formed in the Gulf. Gustav grew to tropical storm strength on Aug. 22, 2008 on its way to western Cuba. Gustav then surged to hurricane strength overnight in the Gulf of Mexico with 75 mph winds. It made its initial landfall in Haiti and the Dominican Republic, where it was responsible for 22 deaths.

Forecasters predicted a strong landfall in Louisiana and Gov. Bobby Jindal issued a state of emergency on Sept. 1. Gustav made landfall that same day as a Category 2 hurricane in Cocodrie, Louisiana with 105 mph winds.

FEMA estimated only 10,000 people remained in New Orleans on September 1 as most residents left under a mandatory evacuation order. Gustav lost strength as it traveled north through Louisiana on Sept. 1 and diminished to a tropical storm by the end of the day.

Nearly 2 million people evacuated from south Louisiana as Gustav approached, and the city of New Orleans did not officially reopen until September 4. New Orleans escaped major damage: It faced mostly street flooding and widespread downed trees. The deaths of 10 people in Louisiana were blamed on the storm. U.S. damage from Hurricane Gustav totaled $4.3 billion, and the name was retired for future hurricane seasons.

Hurricane Ike - September 2008

Hurricane Ike formed quickly between Sept. 1-4, 2008. It became a tropical storm on Sept. 1 in the Atlantic and grew to hurricane strength two days later, before crossing into the Gulf of Mexico. On the afternoon of Sept. 3, Ike intensified to a major hurricane and recorded 145 mph winds.

Ike made its initial landfall in the Caribbean on Sept. 7 as a Category 4 hurricane, producing tropical storm force winds in the Florida Keys. Ike crossed Cuba and entered the Gulf as a Category 1 hurricane. Winds grew to 100 mph and the storm intensified to a Category 2 before making landfall at Galveston Island in Texas.

Ike’s storm surge hit the Louisiana coast before its predicted landfall. Areas still recovering from Gustav’s floods were re-flooded, and more homes were left without power. Hundreds of residents needed rescue and one person was killed in a Terrebonne Parish bayou flood. The third major hurricane of the 2008 season, Ike caused an estimated $29.5 billion in damage in the U.S.

Hurricane Isaac - August 2012

Isaac entered the Gulf of Mexico from the southeast as a tropical storm on Aug. 27, 2012. After crossing through the Florida Keys, Isaac became a hurricane with winds reaching 80 mph around 100 miles south of the Louisiana coast. Rainstorms from Isaac reached New Orleans while the storm was still over the Gulf and before it made its two landfalls.

Hurricane Isaac made two landfalls during its brief stint as a Category 1 hurricane. During the first landfall in Plaquemines Parish, the storm sustained 80 mph winds but was back over the Gulf within three hours. The storm held its Category 1 strength and that same night made another landfall just west of Port Fourchon. The storm crept slowly north on Aug. 29 following its second landfall and weakened until subsiding near Alexandria.

Isaac was categorized by rampant flooding in Louisiana and Mississippi south of I-20. Eighty percent of roads in Franklin County, Miss. roads were blocked by fallen trees. The storm also toppled oil storage containers in Lafitte. By Aug. 30, Isaac had moved almost entirely out of Louisiana on its way northwest into Missouri. The storm caused an estimated $2.39 billion and is the costliest American hurricane to not have its name retired.
